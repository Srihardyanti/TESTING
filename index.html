Most of my professional experience is closely related to instructional design, both in dedicated roles as an Instructional Designer and in adjacent roles such as Quality Assurance and Project Management, where I was responsible for ensuring the quality and effectiveness of learning outputs.

Through working across different organizations, products, and learning formats, I have developed an understanding that instructional design involves both structured principles and contextual flexibility.

On one hand, there are consistent foundations, such as applying established frameworks (e.g. ADDIE), defining clear and measurable learning objectives, structuring content through scaffolding, and designing assessments aligned with cognitive and behavioral principles to support learner comprehension. On the other hand, each project requires adaptation based on factors such as target audience, learning context, product format, and organizational framework.

My quality assurance experience goes beyond proofreading. It involves evaluating whether content, structure, and assessment design are aligned with the intended learning outcomes, source material, and learner needs. In practice, this means identifying weak logic, unclear framing, overcrowded slides, inaccurate generalization, and gaps between material and evaluation.

Selected Quality Review Cases

Case 1 — Unclear Main Claim
Context
This section explained goal attainment as a benefit of effective expense management.
Identified Issue
The opening slide did not clearly communicate the main claim. It introduced supporting ideas without establishing a strong central statement.
Feedback Approach
Recommended refining the opening slide into one concise thesis statement that clearly sets direction for the explanation and avoids introducing support points too early.
Case 2 — Overgeneralized Assessment Statement
Context
True/False question based on statistical data from course material.
Identified Issue
The statement applied findings too broadly, whereas the original source described a narrower context.
Feedback Approach
Emphasized accuracy when translating data into assessment items, avoiding overgeneralization, and ensuring alignment between source material and the assessment statement.
Case 3 — Overcrowded Opening Slide
Context
A slide attempted to introduce multiple perspectives at once but reduced clarity rather than increasing depth.
Identified Issue
The content introduced several viewpoints without enough distinction, reducing instructional value and making the material feel redundant.
Feedback Approach
Suggested either focusing on the most relevant discipline for clarity or retaining multiple perspectives only if each one was clearly differentiated through unique concepts and language.
Case 4 — Structure and Readability Review
Context
Review of opening content intended to establish direction for the lesson.
Identified Issue
The material contained useful information, but hierarchy and framing weakened the learning flow and made the beginning feel heavier than necessary.
Feedback Approach
Reframed the slide sequence to strengthen hierarchy, surface the key idea earlier, and improve readability without discarding the original content.
During my master’s studies, I contributed as a Policy Brief Writer at the Center for Indigenous and Cultural Psychology (CICP), Gadjah Mada University.

In this role, I translated psychology research, particularly on family dynamics and social relationships, into actionable policy recommendations relevant to Indonesian education and employment contexts.

Notes: CICP has dedicated teams for research and policy brief development. To ensure that research findings are actionable, both teams collaborate closely. The process focuses on analyzing the relevance of research findings in relation to supporting evidence, current societal issues, and existing Indonesian government regulations.
