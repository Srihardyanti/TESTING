On the other hand, each project requires adaptation based on factors such as target audience, learning context, product format, and organizational framework.

General QA Process and Analytical Approach

01
Objective Alignment and Context Understanding

The quality assurance process began with understanding the course objective to ensure it clearly reflected the purpose of the product, target audience, and intended learning outcomes. Establishing this foundation was essential to maintain consistency throughout the QA process and to ensure that all feedback aligned with the overall direction of the course.

Analytical Approach: At this stage, I reviewed not only the Instructional Designer's draft but also materials from the Subject Matter Expert (referred to as Researcher in ILMS) to build a comprehensive understanding of the core concepts. This allowed me to:

  • Understand the rationale behind how objectives were formulated
  • Identify potential gaps or blind spots in the design
  • Ensure alignment between conceptual accuracy and instructional intent

In addition to document review, I collaborated closely with the Instructional Designer and Subject Matter Expert to refine the objectives and ensure both contextual and conceptual alignment if needed.

02
Content Review and Quality Evaluation

With clearly defined objectives, the QA process continued with reviewing the course materials. The scope of review varied depending on the product type and associated guidelines. In general, the outputs included:

  • Theory modules (including audio-visual scripts, where applicable)
  • Practice/Case Study modules
  • Quizzes and assessments

Analytical Approach: The review focused on two key dimensions:

Instructional Design quality (not limited to):

  • Conceptual accuracy
  • Content scaffolding and logical progression
  • Alignment between objectives, content, and assessments
  • Appropriate cognitive level of activities and questions
  • Managing cognitive load based on the target audience

Linguistic and communication quality (not limited to):

  • Grammar and language accuracy
  • Clarity and coherence of explanations
  • Consistency in tone and terminology
  • Conciseness and readability
  • Appropriate level of formality for the target audience

These two dimensions served as the foundation for evaluating structure, content quality, and overall cohesiveness.

03
Feedback and Communication Approach

Constructive feedback

Providing feedback was not only about identifying issues, but also about supporting improvement in a structured and collaborative way.

Approach: For issues that were clearly defined within established guidelines, I:

  • Highlighted specific issues in the draft
  • Explained the rationale behind the misalignment
  • Provided alternative solutions where applicable
  • Opened space for discussion to ensure shared understanding

For more complex or context-specific cases that were not fully covered by existing guidelines, I:

  • Applied professional judgment based on instructional design principles
  • Collaborated with the Instructional Designer to explore suitable alternatives
  • Ensured that decisions remained aligned with both learning objectives and learner needs

This approach ensured that feedback was not only corrective, but also constructive and adaptable across different course contexts.

Audio-Visual Script — Theory/Conceptual Module

Audio-visual scripts are a core component of self-paced courses. They include voice-over narration, slide text, and notes for the multimedia team. Each section typically follows a structured flow: Opening slide presenting the main idea or thesis → Supporting slides explaining the "why" and "how" with evidence → Closing slide summarizing key points.

Case 1 — Course A: Overlapping Disciplinary Definitions
Context: This section aimed to define the course topic by presenting perspectives from multiple disciplines (e.g., medical and psychological).
Identified Issues: The definitions from different disciplines were largely overlapping, without clearly highlighting the unique perspective of each field. This reduced the instructional value, as the content did not add meaningful differentiation despite introducing multiple viewpoints.
Feedback Approach
For course A, I highlighted the redundancy and emphasized the importance of ensuring that each piece of information contributes distinct value. I proposed two possible directions: (1) Focus on the most relevant discipline for clarity and conciseness, or (2) Retain multiple perspectives, but clearly differentiate each by emphasizing unique concepts or keywords. This required further alignment with the Subject Matter Expert to ensure both accuracy and clarity.
Case 1 — Course B: Well-Differentiated Perspectives
Context: In another course, definitions from multiple disciplines were clearly differentiated and aligned with the guidelines.
Feedback Approach
In this case, I acknowledged the clarity and effectiveness of the structure by: demonstrating my understanding of the content and highlighting how each perspective contributed uniquely. This approach reinforces good practices and encourages consistency in future outputs.
Case 2 — Weak Thesis Statement
Context: This section explained goal attainment as a benefit of effective expense management.
Identified Issues: The opening slide did not clearly communicate the main claim. Instead, it introduced supporting ideas without establishing a strong central statement.
Feedback Approach
I recommended refining the opening slide to clearly present a single, concise thesis statement that: directly communicates the main idea; sets direction for the subsequent explanation; avoids introducing supporting details prematurely.
Case 3 — Overcrowded Opening Slide
Context: This section introduced a "rhythmic homekeeping system" as a solution for inconsistent home organization.
Identified Issues: The opening slide contained excessive detail and repeated information that was later explained in subsequent slides. This reduced clarity and weakened the distinction between: the thesis (main claim), and the body content (supporting explanation).
Feedback Approach
I suggested refining the opening slide to focus on: a clear and concise claim; minimal explanation; a forward-looking statement that sets expectations. The detailed explanation should be reserved for the subsequent slides, supported by evidence from credible sources.

Case Study

Case studies were designed using H5P format and presenting branching scenarios to encourage critical thinking and application of concepts in real-life situations.

Case Study 1 — Inconsistency & Structural Issues
Context: This case study focused on helping learners identify spending triggers in expense management.
Identified Issues: There were inconsistencies in the scenario (e.g., naming, flow) and redundancy across questions, which disrupted the logical progression of the case. Some questions also lacked a clear purpose in guiding learners toward the intended learning outcome.
Feedback Approach
I identified both surface-level inconsistencies and deeper structural issues, ensuring: logical continuity across the scenario; clear purpose for each question; alignment with learning objectives.
Case Study 2 — Unclear Instructions & Context
Context: This case study focused on applying zone-based scheduling for home organization.
Identified Issues: The instructions and scenario context were not clearly defined, making it difficult for learners to understand: what is expected of them; how to evaluate the answer choices.
Feedback Approach
I emphasized the importance of clarity in: instructions; question context; alignment between questions and answer options. I also referenced a well-executed example from the same Instructional Designer's previous work to: provide a concrete benchmark; reinforce good practices.

Quiz and Assessment

Assessments were designed across various formats, targeting different cognitive levels from understanding to analysis.

Quiz Case 1 — Unbalanced Option Length
Context: Multiple-choice question with four options.
Identified Issues: The correct answer was noticeably more detailed and distinct compared to other options, making it easily identifiable without deep thinking.
Feedback Approach
I recommended balancing the structure and complexity of all answer choices to: reduce guessability; encourage genuine cognitive processing.
Quiz Case 2 — Overgeneralized Statistical Statement
Context: True/False question based on statistical data from the course material.
Identified Issues: The statement overgeneralized the data by applying findings broadly, while the original source specified a more limited context (e.g., specific to women).
Feedback Approach
I highlighted the importance of: maintaining accuracy when translating data into assessment items; avoiding overgeneralization; ensuring alignment between source material and assessment statements.

With a clearer understanding of the issue and its underlying mechanisms, the next step was to identify gaps in existing government regulations.

Guiding question:

Executive Summary
Early paternal involvement plays a critical role in supporting children's cognitive, social, and emotional development, particularly in building strong emotional bonds during the newborn stage. However, in Indonesia, early paternal involvement is often constrained by structural factors, particularly the lack of clearly defined paternal leave policies, which limits opportunities for fathers to engage during this critical period. Therefore, establishing a clear and specific paternal leave policy is essential to support early father and child interaction.
Background
Child developmental challenges in Indonesia, including behavioral issues and social difficulties, remain a significant concern. Research highlights the importance of family dynamics, particularly the role of fathers, in shaping children's developmental outcomes. Despite this, existing social and institutional systems have not fully supported active paternal involvement, especially during the early stages of a child's life. This creates a need to examine how current structures, including workplace policies and government regulations, influence parenting practices.
Scientific Evidence
Research indicates that limited paternal involvement during early childhood is associated with weaker emotional bonding and reduced openness between father and child, increasing the risk of behavioral and developmental challenges later in life. Trust plays a central role in this relationship, developing through consistent and meaningful interaction. When such interaction is limited, the formation of trust and emotional connection is constrained. These interaction patterns are shaped not only by individual factors, but also by structural conditions. In the absence of clearly defined paternal leave policies, fathers have fewer opportunities to engage during the newborn stage, limiting the potential for early bonding.
Implication: Strategic Direction
Addressing this issue requires a systemic approach that integrates public awareness, policy reform, and cross-sector collaboration. Strengthening support for early paternal involvement should be positioned as part of a broader effort to improve child development outcomes in Indonesia.
Recommendation: Actionable Steps
  1. Increase public awareness of the importance of father involvement in early parenting.
  2. Revise National Civil Service Agency Regulation No. 24 of 2017 to explicitly include paternal leave.
  3. Provide support mechanisms for civil servant families to ensure effective implementation of paternal leave.
  4. Encourage collaboration between government and industry to ensure that paternal leave policies are practical and sustainable.
